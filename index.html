Profile · Investment Banking & Culture

The Analyst Who Writes What Finance Refuses to Say

"I'm most alive when I'm asking the question nobody in the room wanted to ask."

By The Gazette Desk · New York City

Riya Goel is a contradiction that Wall Street hasn't figured out what to do with yet. She is an Investment Banking Analyst at Bank of America's Consumer & Retail Group — and she is also the person who wrote "Kissing My Resume for the Last Time," a Medium essay that reads like a quiet manifesto against everything her day job represents.

She graduated from Barnard College in December 2024, a semester ahead of schedule, with a degree in Economics and Data Science. Before she touched a financial model, she was a United Nations Youth Consultant at sixteen, advocating for gender equity on the world stage. At nineteen, she published her first book — during a pandemic.

At twenty-one, she founded Barnard Investment Group, the first women-focused investment club at Barnard College, expanding its reach to include financial literacy programs in Harlem. She then worked as a gallery associate at High Line Nine, one of New York's premier gallery complexes — simultaneously.

Her writing on Medium spans fashion as cultural critique, South Asian identity in predominantly white institutions, and the aesthetics of political drift in fast fashion. None of it reads like it was written by someone who works at an investment bank. That's precisely the point.

Finance, fashion, and the South Asian diaspora's relationship to power — someone has to write about it honestly.
