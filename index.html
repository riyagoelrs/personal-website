Profile · Investment Banking & Culture

The Analyst Who Writes What Finance Refuses to Say

"I'm most alive when I'm asking the question nobody in the room wanted to ask."

By The Gazette Desk · New York City

Riya Goel is a contradiction that Wall Street hasn't figured out what to do with yet. She is an Investment Banking Analyst at Bank of America's Consumer & Retail Group — and she is also the person who wrote "Kissing My Resume for the Last Time," a Medium essay that reads like a quiet manifesto against everything her day job represents.

She graduated from Barnard College in December 2024, a semester ahead of schedule, with a degree in Economics and Data Science. Before she touched a financial model, she was a United Nations Youth Consultant at sixteen, advocating for gender equity on the world stage. At nineteen, she published her first book — during a pandemic.

At twenty-one, she founded Barnard Investment Group, the first women-focused investment club at Barnard College, expanding its reach to include financial literacy programs in Harlem. She then worked as a gallery associate at High Line Nine, one of New York's premier gallery complexes — simultaneously.

Her writing on Medium spans fashion as cultural critique, South Asian identity in predominantly white institutions, and the aesthetics of political drift in fast fashion. None of it reads like it was written by someone who works at an investment bank. That's precisely the point.

Finance, fashion, and the South Asian diaspora's relationship to power — someone has to write about it honestly.

Textile Documentary Project
In Development · Documentary Film

Thread, Unraveled

A short documentary exploring the lives of South Asian textile workers — and the Western fashion industry that profits from their invisibility.

Fast fashion has a face. It's just never been the face on the billboard. This documentary project traces the supply chain of a single garment — from loom to label — following the workers, mostly women, whose labor makes global fashion possible but who remain structurally excluded from its rewards and its visibility.

The project sits at the intersection of everything I've been writing about for years: the aesthetics of power, South Asian identity, and what it means for a diaspora community to consume the very culture it is descended from. It is not a polemic. It is a portrait.

I came to this through the art world — a year at High Line Nine taught me how cultural objects acquire and shed value, and who gets to decide. Textiles are cultural objects. They are also labor. That tension is the film.

Currently in research and pre-production. If you are a cinematographer, producer, or someone working at this intersection — reach out.

The Brand
In Development · Personal Brand

On Building Something

At the intersection of South Asian aesthetics, finance culture, and fashion criticism — a brand that takes all three seriously and apologizes for none of them.

01
The Aesthetic

South Asian visual traditions are among the most sophisticated in the world. They deserve to be treated as design language — rigorous, contemporary, and entirely non-apologetic — not as trend, not as costume. The brand starts here.

02
The Voice

Financial literacy. Cultural criticism. Identity writing. The brand's editorial voice moves between these registers without getting stuck in any of them. It speaks to women who work in rooms where they are the only one — and who are building their way out of that situation, on their own terms.

03
The Audience

South Asian women in finance and professional services. The diaspora navigating Western institutions without flattening themselves to fit. Anyone who has been told their interests are too contradictory to be taken seriously — and refused to simplify.

04
The Build

Currently in early development. Content, community, and eventually product. If you're a designer, strategist, or someone building at this intersection — the conversation is open. Reach out via the contact section below.
